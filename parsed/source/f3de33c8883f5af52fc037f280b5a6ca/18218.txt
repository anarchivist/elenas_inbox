 Proposed Smoking Cessation Program as Part of the Tobacco Settlement.
You inquired about a recent study in the Journal of the American Medical Association
regarding the implementation of a smoking cessation guideline. The study found that full
implementation of the BBS guideline to screen all patients for tobacco use and provide
cessation assistance to all willing patients would cost $6.3 billion in the first year and would
achieve approximately 1.7 million new quitters over and above a baseline quit rate. The
study estimated that these results compare favorably with the costs of other preventative
intervention programs such as mammography screening. On September 17, you listed
expansion of smoking cessation programs as one of the elements that must be included in
any national tobacco legislation. The tobacco settlement proposed that $1 billion for the
first 4 years and $1.5 billion thereafter would be used to assist individuals who want to quit
using tobacco. With respect to the study's conclusions, BBS and OMB believe that there
are other methods for implementing smoking cessation programs that might have lower
costs such as providing incentives to managed-care plans to offer smoking cessation
programs. As part of the legislative process, BBS is preparing a list of cessation resource
allocation options and how many quitters each option may produce. They caution that
this is a very inexact science.
